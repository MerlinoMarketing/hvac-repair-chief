# AC Drain Line Clogs in Florida: Prevention, Cleaning, and When to Call

In South Florida, AC drain line clogs aren't an occasional inconvenience—they're a near-universal problem that every homeowner will face. Our climate creates perfect conditions: constantly running AC systems, high ambient humidity, and warm temperatures that turbocharge algae and mold growth in standing water.

A clogged drain line shuts down your AC and, if the float switch fails or isn't installed, can cause significant water damage to ceilings, walls, and flooring. This guide covers everything you need to know.

## How the Condensate Drain System Works

When your AC runs, the evaporator coil gets cold and moisture from your home's air condenses on it—just like condensation on a cold glass. That water drips into the condensate drain pan below the coil and flows out through the drain line.

In South Florida, a typical residential system removes 5–20 gallons of water per day from the air. All of that water travels through the condensate drain line to the outside of your home (usually to a floor drain, laundry sink, or exterior drain).

The drain line is typically 3/4" PVC pipe. It's a dark, warm, moist environment—ideal for algae, mold, and sludge buildup.

## Signs of a Drain Line Clog

| Symptom | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| AC shuts off randomly | Float switch triggered by full drain pan |
| Water pooling under air handler | Pan overflowed (float switch may have failed) |
| Ceiling stains or water spots | Drain pan overflow in attic-mounted units |
| Musty smell from vents | Standing water and algae growth |
| High humidity indoors | AC not running due to float switch shutoff |

## DIY Drain Line Maintenance (Monthly)

### The Bleach Method

The simplest and most effective preventive maintenance:

1. Locate the drain line access point (a capped PVC tee fitting near the air handler)
2. Remove the cap
3. Pour 1/4 cup of diluted bleach (1 part bleach to 16 parts water) or white distilled vinegar into the opening
4. Replace the cap
5. Repeat monthly

This kills algae and mold before they accumulate enough to clog the line.

### Drain Line Access Tablets

Algaecide tablets designed for condensate drain pans are available at HVAC supply houses and home improvement stores. Drop one in the drain pan monthly. They dissolve slowly and treat the pan and line for 30–90 days.

### Wet-Vac Method (When There's a Clog)

If you suspect a clog:

1. Find the drain line outlet (usually on an exterior wall or near the foundation)
2. Use a wet/dry shop vacuum—press the nozzle firmly over the drain opening
3. Create a seal with your hand or a rag
4. Run the vacuum for 2–3 minutes
5. This often pulls the clog out immediately

**Success indicator:** You'll hear a "pop" when the clog clears, and water will flow freely.

## What Your HVAC Technician Uses

For stubborn or recurring clogs, professionals use:

- **Nitrogen purge** — Pressurized nitrogen blown through the line clears even severe blockages
- **Wet-vac with commercial attachments** — More powerful than consumer models
- **Drain line inspection** — Camera inspection for damaged or disconnected lines
- **Chemical treatment** — Professional-grade algaecide treatment after clearing

## When to Call a Professional

DIY methods work well for prevention and minor clogs. Call a technician when:

- DIY methods haven't cleared the clog after two attempts
- Water has already overflowed and you have ceiling or wall damage
- You can't locate the drain line access point or outlet
- The drain pan is corroded or cracked (needs replacement)
- Your float switch isn't working (system doesn't shut off when pan is full)
- You have recurring clogs more than twice per year despite maintenance

## Understanding Your Float Switch

The float switch is a safety device installed in the drain pan that shuts off the AC when the water level rises too high. It prevents overflow and water damage.

**Testing your float switch:**
1. With the system running, manually lift the float (a small plastic device in the drain pan)
2. The system should shut off within seconds
3. Release the float—system should restart

If the float switch doesn't shut the system off when lifted, it may be failed or disconnected. This is a significant risk for water damage and should be repaired immediately.

## Attic Air Handler Drain Considerations

Homes with air handlers in the attic face higher risk from drain clogs:

- A clogged drain in an attic unit means water drips onto your ceiling—expensive damage
- Secondary drain pans should be installed below attic air handlers (required by Florida code)
- Some attic air handlers have a secondary float switch in the overflow pan as additional protection

If you have an attic-mounted air handler, consider a visual inspection of the drain pan at every filter change (every 30–45 days).

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often do drain lines clog in South Florida?**
A: Without preventive maintenance, South Florida AC drain lines typically clog 1–3 times per year. With monthly maintenance (bleach or tablets), most homeowners prevent clogs entirely.

**Q: Can I use Drano or drain cleaner on my AC drain line?**
A: No. Harsh chemical drain cleaners can damage PVC fittings and seals, and can corrode the drain pan. Diluted bleach, vinegar, or HVAC-specific algaecide are the right choices.

**Q: My AC shut off and the drain pan has water—what do I do?**
A: Attempt the wet-vac method to clear the line, then empty or blot the pan dry. Restart the system. If it shuts off again quickly, the float switch is working but there's still a clog—call a technician.

**Q: Is my secondary drain line the one I see dripping outside?**
A: Yes. If you see water dripping from a secondary drain line (sometimes located higher up on an exterior wall than the primary), your primary drain line is clogged and the pan is overflowing. This is normal operation for the safety system—but the primary clog needs to be cleared.
