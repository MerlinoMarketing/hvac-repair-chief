# Why Your AC Isn't Cooling Enough in Florida (And How to Fix It)

In South Florida, an air conditioner that runs all day but can't cool your home past 80°F isn't just an inconvenience—it's a real problem. And it's one of the most common service calls we get in Boca Raton during summer months.

The good news: most cases of insufficient cooling have identifiable causes and clear solutions. Here's a systematic guide to diagnosing and fixing the problem.

## Step 1: Rule Out Simple Issues First

Before calling a technician, check these basics:

**Air filter:** A severely clogged filter restricts airflow and dramatically reduces cooling capacity. Replace it if it's been more than 30–45 days, and check if cooling improves within an hour.

**Thermostat settings:** Verify it's set to COOL (not HEAT or FAN only) and to a temperature below the current indoor reading. Check batteries.

**Circuit breaker:** A partially tripped breaker can cause the air handler to run without the outdoor unit operating. Check your electrical panel.

**Vents:** Confirm all supply vents are open and unobstructed. Blocked vents dramatically reduce room cooling.

## Common Causes of Insufficient Cooling in Florida

### 1. Low Refrigerant (Refrigerant Leak)

Refrigerant is the medium that moves heat from inside your home to the outside. Low refrigerant—always due to a leak, since refrigerant doesn't "get used up"—means reduced cooling capacity.

**Signs:**
- Ice forming on refrigerant lines or evaporator coil
- System running constantly without reaching setpoint
- Warm air from supply vents
- Hissing or bubbling sounds near the refrigerant lines

**Fix:** A technician must find and repair the leak, then recharge the system. Simply "topping off" without fixing the leak is a bandaid—the refrigerant will escape again.

### 2. Dirty Evaporator or Condenser Coils

Dirty coils reduce heat transfer efficiency significantly. The evaporator coil (indoor) collects dust and mold in humid climates. The condenser coil (outdoor) collects dirt, grass, and pollen.

**Signs:**
- System runs longer than normal to reach setpoint
- Higher than usual electric bills
- Visible dirt on accessible coils

**Fix:** Professional coil cleaning. The condenser can be rinsed with a garden hose monthly as preventive maintenance.

### 3. Failing Compressor

The compressor is the heart of your AC system. A failing compressor may run but provide reduced cooling capacity—a subtle symptom that gets worse over time until the compressor fails completely.

**Signs:**
- System runs but can only cool 5–10°F above setpoint
- Clicking or grinding from the outdoor unit
- Compressor is hot to the touch (beyond normal)
- Electrician-measured amperage is high

**Fix:** Compressor replacement ($1,500–$2,500+ for the part alone) or system replacement. On systems over 8 years old, replacement is usually more economical.

### 4. Undersized System

An air conditioner that's too small for your home simply cannot keep up with South Florida's heat load on peak days. This is a design problem, not a maintenance issue.

**Signs:**
- System runs 100% of the time on hot days (90°F+) but still can't cool the home
- System was recently replaced and cooling is worse than before
- Recent addition or renovation increased the home's square footage

**Fix:** Have a Manual J load calculation performed. If the system is undersized, upgrade to proper capacity.

### 5. Duct Leaks

Supply duct leaks allow conditioned air to escape into the attic before reaching living spaces. The system produces cold air—it just never arrives at your rooms.

**Signs:**
- Specific rooms are consistently harder to cool
- Attic is unusually cold
- System runs constantly
- Air registers have reduced airflow

**Fix:** Professional duct inspection and sealing with mastic or Aeroseal.

### 6. Poor Home Insulation

In South Florida's climate, inadequate attic insulation allows tremendous heat transfer into the living space. Even a perfectly functioning HVAC system can't overcome a poorly insulated attic.

**Signs:**
- Home cools fine at night but struggles during peak afternoon heat
- Second floor is significantly hotter than first floor
- Attic insulation is thin, compressed, or missing in areas

**Fix:** Add attic insulation to R-38 to R-60 (Florida recommended levels).

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How many degrees should AC cool below outdoor temperature in Florida?**
A: A properly functioning AC system should be able to maintain indoor temperature 20–25°F below outdoor temperature. On a 95°F day, your home should reach 70–75°F without difficulty. If you can only achieve 80°F indoors on hot days, something is wrong.

**Q: Why does my AC cool well at night but not during the day?**
A: Daytime heat loads are much higher due to solar gain through windows and roof. If your system is marginally sized, undersized, or has reduced capacity (refrigerant, coil, or compressor issues), it may struggle only during peak demand hours.

**Q: My AC is running but only blowing slightly cool air—what's wrong?**
A: This pattern most commonly indicates low refrigerant or a dirty evaporator coil. Both reduce the coil's ability to absorb heat. Call an HVAC technician for diagnosis.

**Q: Can I recharge AC refrigerant myself?**
A: No. EPA regulations require HVAC technicians to be EPA 608 certified to purchase and handle refrigerants. DIY refrigerant kits are not legal for typical HVAC refrigerants and can cause system damage.
